Final Reflection

The most straightforward part of this project was finding ways on how to make both sides compelling utilizing the same data set. The data already contained multiple possible stories, one about long term historical damage and another about recent annual damage. Because of this, we did not need to fabricate any values or use false information. Instead, the vast majority of persuasion came from choosing different time ranges, different transformations, different titles, and different color emphasis.

The most difficult part was deciding where persuasion turns into misleading. For instance, using cumulative data for the “for” side is reasonable because the proposition is about history. However, emphasizing the United States with color and annotations while China still leads in total cumulative CO2 damage which creates a more persuasive chart. Similarly, highlighting only 2019 data for the “against” side makes China look clearly dominant, but it also ignores the larger historical context. This showed us that deception in visualization is often not about making up data, but about choosing which truthful parts of the data to emphasize.

Ethical visualization is therefore defined as visualization that is transparent enough for the viewer to comprehend what is being visualized, while still allowing the designer to make an argument. Ethical visualization becomes misleading when they hide important context that would substantially change the viewer’s interpretation. In our project, the most ethical choices involved clear labels, sourcing information, and time spans that were appropriate for the visualization. The more questionable choices were vague titles, selective time periods, and visual emphasis that directed attention away from inconvenient parts of the data.
