Project 2 Persuasive or Deceptive Visualization?

Prabhjas Singh, Edison Aryan, Tanvi Vidyala — prs018@ucsd.edu, edaryan@ucsd.edu, tvidyala@ucsd.edu

Proposition: The United States has caused more CO2 economic damage than any other country except China throughout modern industrial history.

Our source was the World Bank Human Development Indicators dataset, specifically the environment.csv file. We used the same dataset for both sides of the proposition, but transformed and framed the data differently to create two opposing persuasive arguments.

FOR the Proposition

Design Decisions and Rationale

Data Types

  • X-Axis: Q-interval (Years, 1970–2019)
  • Y-Axis: Q-ratio (Annual CO2 Damage in Billions of USD)

Visual Encoding Choices

  • Title and Narrative Framing
    • Score: -1.5
    • The title encodes the narrative framing to prime the viewer's interpretation before they read the data. It centers the US's prolonged dominance as the story, which draws attention away from the more recent and dramatic trend of China surpassing and far exceeding US damage levels. A neutral title would present both countries' trajectories without a leading argument.
  • Color (Bold Red for the US)
    • Score: -1
    • Color draws attention to the US's line using a bold, distinct color, directing viewer focus toward the country the argument centers on while leaving other countries visually secondary. Despite this, China's line ultimately rises higher, so the visual hierarchy conflicts with the actual data hierarchy, which is a mildly deceptive tradeoff. Using equal visual weight for all countries was considered but would have undermined the persuasive focus.
  • Shaded Region and In-Plot Annotation
    • Score: -1.5
    • The shaded background and annotation ("US was #1 for ~35 years") directly label the historical period of US dominance, reinforcing the key message. This decision emphasizes the time period where the United States dominated CO2 damage, which reinforces the argument while downplaying more recent trends. Placing the annotation outside the plot was considered but would have reduced its visual integration with the data.
  • Line Styles for Secondary Countries
    • Score: 0.5
    • Different line styles differentiate secondary countries without introducing additional colors, keeping visual complexity low and focus on the US. This is a largely neutral choice that reduces clutter. Although it also subtly reinforces the hierarchy by making India and Russia feel like footnotes rather than relevant comparisons to China and the US.

Deceptive Techniques Used:

  • Draws attention to the United States's CO2 damage levels through the title and the usage of bold color to highlight the US's line. However, despite this, the data actually shows that China has caused more damage overall.
  • Emphasizes the time period where the United States dominated CO2 damage, which reinforces the argument while downplaying more recent trends.

AGAINST the Proposition

Design Decisions and Rationale

Data Types

  • X-Axis: Q-interval (Years, 1990–2019)
  • Y-Axis: Q-ratio (CO2 Damage Index, normalized to 1990 = 100)

Visual Encoding Choices

  • Title and Narrative Framing
    • Score: -1.5
    • The title encodes the narrative framing to guide the viewer's interpretation before they engage with the data. It centers China's dramatic growth as the story while describing US levels as "relatively stable," which downplays the fact that the US still nearly tripled its damage index over the same period. The title also keeps the time range vague, obscuring that this uses only annual 2019 data rather than cumulative CO2 damage.
  • Indexed Scale (Data Transformation)
    • Score: -1.5
    • The y-axis uses an indexed scale normalized to 1990 = 100, which emphasizes growth rates rather than absolute values. This makes China's increase appear more dramatic relative to the US than it would on an absolute scale, since China started from a much lower baseline in 1990. A neutral alternative would have used absolute damage values, but that would have undermined the argument about relative growth.
  • Area Fill Between Lines
    • Score: -1
    • The area fill between the two lines highlights the growing gap, making the divergence more noticeable. This amplifies the magnitude of China's growth by drawing the eye to the widening space rather than to either country's individual trajectory. Lines without fill would have been more neutral and less immediately striking.
  • Color (Orange for China, Blue for the US)
    • Score: 0.5
    • Orange encodes China and blue encodes the United States, using color hue as an identity channel to distinguish the two nominal categories. This pairing makes each country's trajectory easy to track across the full time range. The choice is largely earnest as a legibility decision.

Deceptive Techniques Used:

  • Only used CO2 damage data from 2019 instead of cumulative CO2 damage. However, the title keeps this vague and doesn't mention the time range of the data.
  • Uses an indexed scale, which exaggerates China's rapid growth compared to the United States, emphasizing growth trends rather than absolute values, making China's increase appear more dramatic.

Final Reflection

The most straightforward part of this project was finding ways on how to make both sides compelling utilizing the same data set. The data already contained multiple possible stories, one about long term historical damage and another about recent annual damage. Because of this, we did not need to fabricate any values or use false information. Instead, the vast majority of persuasion came from choosing different time ranges, different transformations, different titles, and different color emphasis.

The most difficult part was deciding where persuasion turns into misleading. For instance, using cumulative data for the “for” side is reasonable because the proposition is about history. However, emphasizing the United States with color and annotations while China still leads in total cumulative CO2 damage which creates a more persuasive chart. Similarly, highlighting only 2019 data for the “against” side makes China look clearly dominant, but it also ignores the larger historical context. This showed us that deception in visualization is often not about making up data, but about choosing which truthful parts of the data to emphasize.

Ethical visualization is therefore defined as visualization that is transparent enough for the viewer to comprehend what is being visualized, while still allowing the designer to make an argument. Ethical visualization becomes misleading when they hide important context that would substantially change the viewer’s interpretation. In our project, the most ethical choices involved clear labels, sourcing information, and time spans that were appropriate for the visualization. The more questionable choices were vague titles, selective time periods, and visual emphasis that directed attention away from inconvenient parts of the data.
